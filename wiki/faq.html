Index of Social Responsibility (ISR)

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Doesn't ISR just recreate inequality?

A: No. ISR makes inequality transparent and justified. Unlike popularity contests or top-down hierarchies, ISR weights are based on:

  • Expertise relevant to the specific issue (not general power)
  • Proven commitment to community values (not inherited status)
  • Direct relevance to the decision (not blanket authority)
All members can see how scores are calculated and can build influence through contribution.

Q: What if someone disagrees with how their ISR is calculated?

A: ISR is fully transparent. Members can:

  • See exactly which factors contributed to their score
  • Ask the AI assistant for explanations
  • Provide additional information about expertise or experience
  • Appeal to the community if they believe calculations are unfair

Q: Can ISR be manipulated?

A: ISR has built-in safeguards:

  • Contribution scores require consistent, long-term alignment with values
  • Sudden changes in behavior or values declarations trigger system warnings
  • Expertise is cross-checked against verifiable credentials
  • Community can adjust weighting formula if they detect gaming

Q: Is ISR only for large organizations?

A: No. ISR scales from small communities (10 members) to large networks (100,000+ members). Really helpful it begans from 150+ members (the Dunbar's number). Where, on the one hand, social dynamics become complex enough to need structured decision-making because of values diversity growing, on the other hand, participants cannot easily behold, keep in mind and take into account all the skills and actions of other participants.

Q: How do we set community values?

A: Matrix of Values is flexible and member-driven. It gathers out of all microcommunity members' inputs:

  • Each community member defines its values in Profile in a convinient way
  • AI assistant helps translate values into operational definitions and, vice-versa, helps to understand what the system means by this definitions
  • Values can be adjusted over time
  • All members can see how their actions align with stated values

Q: How does ISR prevent social fragmentation and encourage participation?

A: ISR addresses atomization by creating dynamic microcommunities:

  • Microcommunities are formed around specific issues (dreams, questions, problems), not static social groups - it insures real actual involvement into the process
  • Membership changes as problems evolve, keeping engagement fresh
  • Transparent decision-making builds trust in open communication
  • Absence of bureaucratic barriers allows instant community formation when needed
  • Every member has fair influence levers regardless of background
This counters the "internal emigration" tendency by making participation rewarding and meaningful.

Q: What about privacy? Does ISR track all my actions?

A: ISR respects privacy while ensuring accountability:

  • Only actions affecting others are considered "social actions" and can be evaluated
  • Information access is tiered based on relevance using Adaptable authorization (family, doctor, neighbor have different access levels)
  • Healthy community dynamics reduce the need for anonymity by creating safe expression spaces
The goal is not surveillance, but creating environments where people feel secure being authentic.

Q: How does ISR differ from money-based systems?

A: ISR addresses limitations of monetary systems:

  • Money measures everything in monetary terms, but ISR measures social contribution
  • Money can be acquired without social value creation; ISR requires actual contribution
  • ISR weights are context-specific while money provides blanket influence
  • Money creates incentives to accumulate regardless of method; ISR rewards aligned actions
ISR complements economic systems by adding a social contribution dimension to influence.

Q: Can't AI make mistakes in evaluating human behavior?

A: ISR's approach to AI evaluation is designed for fairness:

  • We're already constantly evaluated by others, exams, certifications - ISR just makes it transparent
  • The evaluation algorithm is open for inspection and adjustment by the community
  • Historical data shows AI often outperforms humans in consistent pattern recognition
  • Multiple perspectives (community input + AI analysis) reduce individual biases
  • All evaluations can be discussed, contested, and refined by the community
The system evolves through collective intelligence, not rigid AI imposition.

Q: How does ISR handle different types of community members?

A: ISR's flexible architecture accommodates diverse participation:

  • Experts: Higher weights in their domain areas
  • Active contributors: Build influence through consistent participation
  • New members: Start with baseline weights that grow with contribution
  • Occasional participants: Influence limited to areas they engage with
  • Special cases: Parents, retirees, students receive contextual consideration
This mirrors real-world social dynamics where influence is earned, not assumed.

Q: What prevents ISR from becoming another controlling bureaucracy?

A: ISR's design prevents bureaucratic calcification:

  • Algorithm is transparent and community-adjustable (like adjusting radio settings)
  • No pre-set values - each community defines its own values
  • Microcommunities dissolve when their purpose is fulfilled
  • Weight calculations are issue-specific, not permanent status assignments
  • System adapts as community values and needs evolve
ISR is a tool, not a ruler - the community determines how it's used.

Q: How does ISR ensure representation of minority views?

A: ISR protects minority participation through:

  • Direct relevance weighting gives niche experts influence in their domains
  • Microcommunity formation around specific concerns prevents majoritarian domination
  • Values declaration ensures diverse perspectives are recognized in community ethos
  • Transparent algorithms allow identification and correction of bias
  • Historical contribution recognition prevents "tyranny of the majority"
The goal is constructive diversity, not homogenized consensus.
