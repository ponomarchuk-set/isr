Computing the Objective: Algorithmic Limits, Social Friction, and the Liberation of Human Agency

Sergii Ponomarchuk - July 2026

Abstract

A persistent illusion of digital governance attempts is the belief that advanced artificial intelligence can measure or simulate the existential structure of human being (Dasein). This paper argues that such attempts commit a fatal ontological error by forcing human authenticity into the calculable, objective realm of Vorhandenheit (presence-at-hand). Rather than striving to make technology more "human," this paper proposes a radical architectural inversion: computational systems succeed politically precisely when they embrace their non-human limits. By explicitly delegating the statistical, bureaucratic, and structural friction of social coordination — conceptualized here through Heidegger’s notion of Das Man — to transparent algorithmic infrastructures like the Index of Social Responsibility (ISR), human attention is fundamentally liberated. When machines absorb the exhausting mechanics of everyday administration that otherwise fuel political apathy and demagogic populism, they strip away the psychological refuge of everyday anonymity. Operating as a ready-to-hand (Zuhandenes) exoskeleton for issue-specific microcommunities, this architecture clears a digital analogue of Hannah Arendt’s "Space of Appearance." Ultimately, the paper concludes that the highest political function of computation is not to govern human beings, but to automate the objective layers of interaction, thereby preserving the space for resoluteness (Entschlossenheit) and authentic human agency.

Keywords: Computational Sociology; Heideggerian Ontology; Digital Governance; Artificial Intelligence; Deliberative Democracy; Index of Social Responsibility (ISR); Human Agency; Dasein; Das Man.

1. Introduction: The Ontological Illusion of AI and Digital Governance

A persistent illusion in contemporary digital governance is that advanced algorithms — including complex and non-linear Large Language Models — can comprehend or measure the existential structure of Dasein. This assumption commits a fundamental ontological error. Technology, regardless of its computational sophistication, cannot interact directly with Dasein (human existence).

Fundamentally, technological systems remain bound to the realm of Vorhandenheit — the objective presence of things. Algorithms operate exclusively through categories, vectors, and statistical calculations. Because a machine inherently "sees things," any attempt to force an algorithm to directly measure Dasein inevitably fails, reducing the human subject to a mere object.

This ontological distinction forms the philosophical foundation of approaches such as the Index of Social Responsibility (ISR), an applied computational sociology framework. From this perspective, the goal of computational sociology is not to replace political judgment with calculation, but to map the objective conditions of interaction. Rather than attempting to overcome this ontological limit, digital architecture should purposefully embrace it. Technology should be deployed to maximize the accounting of the objective, "thing-like" features of human interaction. By automatically managing these categorical and calculable aspects, the system ceases its attempt to quantify human Dasein itself. Consequently, this architectural separation liberates the individual's attention, focusing it entirely on the authentic manifestation of their own Dasein through the initiation, deliberation, decision-making, and implementation of social interactions.

2. The Burden of Das Man and Social Friction

In everyday life, when individuals attempt to reach agreements or make collective decisions, a vast amount of human energy is consumed by structural friction. This friction consists of clarifying objective statuses, evaluating competence, combating manipulation, and navigating bureaucracy. These elements are inherently "thing-like"; they are categorical aspects of interaction that belong entirely to the realm of Vorhandenheit.

Philosophically, this domain of calculated social mechanics aligns with Heidegger’s concept of Das Man (the "They"). It is the realm of the averaged and the stereotypical, dictating "how things are done," what the rules are, and what the statistics say.

However, a rigorous computational sociology must acknowledge that these "objective" metrics are never purely neutral laws of physics. The parameters defining competence, relevance, or contribution are socially constructed; they are formalized agreements of a community’s normative choices, where metrics inevitably risk reproducing existing social realities and power dynamics. Yet, when individuals are forced to expend their resources actively managing these categorical metrics, they are compelled to operate as objects. Crucially, this is often a zero-sum dynamic: structural friction does not merely make coordination inefficient; it frequently consumes all available human energy.

When the entirety of a community's capacity is exhausted by administrative navigation, individuals never reach the stage of authentic, non-mechanical interaction. Two profound consequences emerge from this exhaustion. First is a pervasive collapse of motivation for political action, as individuals retreat from a public sphere that feels purely bureaucratic. Second is the rise of populism. From this ontological perspective, demagogic populism can be interpreted not as an expression of authentic political will, but rather as a highly specialized political strategy that exploits the mechanisms of Das Man. The populist thrives by weaponizing averaged stereotypes, amplifying Gerede (chatter), and exploiting the very friction that exhausts genuine deliberation.

Ultimately, the burden of navigating the objective, averaged mechanics of Das Man completely traps human interaction within Vorhandenheit. This friction is not just an external imposition, but the comfortable, default anonymity of everyday life — a psychological refuge where individuals flee to avoid the anxiety of genuine choice. We must actively architect systems to transcend this tranquilizing state. By stripping away these administrative hiding places, the digital infrastructure forces the space open for what Heidegger termed Entschlossenheit (resoluteness): the courage to step out of the anonymous crowd and assume authentic, personal responsibility for collective action.

3. The Algorithm as Zuhandenheit (Readiness-to-Hand)

To resolve the friction of Das Man, the architectural role of the algorithm must shift. When a computational framework, such as the Index of Social Responsibility (ISR), assumes the burden of tracking interactions and objective metrics, its goal is to become what Heidegger termed Zuhandenes — a ready-to-hand tool.

However, phenomenologically, readiness-to-hand is not a default property of an object; it is forged through mastery and trust. Like a hammer in the hands of a skilled artisan, the digital instrument disappears into the flow of work only when the user has integrated it. Furthermore, even the master routinely exercises circumspection (Umsicht) — briefly bringing the tool into Vorhandenheit (presence-at-hand) to verify its integrity before use. This momentary inspection does not destroy the tool's utility; rather, it guarantees its reliability.

The algorithmic architecture must mirror this lifecycle. Arthur C. Clarke famously observed that advanced technology is indistinguishable from magic. Yet, ontologically, this "magic" is merely the perception of the passive consumer — the alienated subject of Das Man who accepts the outputs of a system without the intention or desire to understand them. By design, systems like the ISR are not opaque "black boxes"; their underlying mechanics are entirely explainable. The barrier to understanding is rarely systemic secrecy, but rather the everyday complacency of the user. Because the system's metrics and historical logs are structurally transparent, any member with the initiative can pull the algorithm back into Vorhandenheit to dispel the illusion of magic and inspect its mechanics. Paradoxically, this permanent capacity for verification — the open invitation to step out of passivity and look under the hood — is exactly what builds the trust necessary for the technology to fade into the background, operating as a silent, invisible foundation for social action.

4. Delegating the Objective World to the Machine

This architectural shift relies on a deliberate delegation: the averaged, calculated, and stereotypical elements of social coordination are explicitly handed over to the machine. Large Language Models serve as the ideal embodiment of this objective world. Operating by predicting the most statistically probable sequences based on the vast aggregate of human discourse, LLMs are the mathematical formalization of Das Man and its Gerede ("chatter") — the anonymous circulation of "what is said" devoid of authentic existential stake.

Traditionally, this statistical averaging is framed as AI's greatest philosophical limitation. Within this architecture, however, it becomes its precise functional specification. The mandate is simple: let the machine embody the averaged world in order to free the human from it. By transferring the dictates of Das Man to the algorithm, the digital framework functions not as a panopticon attempting to quantify the human subject, but as a reliable exoskeleton for microcommunities.

By flawlessly absorbing the burden of statistics and categorical rules, this explicit delegation affords individuals the luxury of stepping outside the confines of anonymous social friction to transcend mere administrative existence. Ultimately, this reveals a fundamental inversion of the prevailing artificial intelligence narrative. Rather than striving to make technology increasingly "human-like," computational governance succeeds precisely by designing machines that are emphatically not human — and realizing that their highest function lies exactly in that ontological limit.

5. Liberating Dasein: The Space for Authentic Action

When the algorithm successfully absorbs the administrative burden and the accounting of objective features, human attention is fundamentally liberated. Relieved from the necessity of navigating systemic friction or proving their categorical weight, individuals enter a cleared political domain. While Habermas might envision this as a prerequisite for an uncoerced communicative space aimed at rational consensus, it functions more fundamentally as a digital analogue of Arendt's "Space of Appearance" — an inherently pluralistic, agonistic domain where meanings inevitably collide, and where individuals encounter one another not as datasets, but as unique existences.

Crucially, this architecture is designed to serve "microcommunities." In this context, a microcommunity is not constrained by static demographic size or traditional electoral geography; rather, it is an issue-specific formation governed by the All-Affected Principle. The exact operationalization of this principle — dynamically computing the boundaries of the affected public — is handled by the underlying algorithmic infrastructure. Depending on the scope of the shared concern, this functional space could theoretically scale to encompass all of humanity.

Within this cleared space, Dasein is realized not through the calculation of status, but in authentic existential acts. This authentic manifestation occurs precisely in the capacity to initiate something genuinely new that an algorithm cannot predict; in live deliberation, where meanings inevitably collide and are actively revised; and in implementation, where individuals assume personal responsibility for collective decisions.

To achieve this, the computational mechanism of the Index of Social Responsibility must be carefully restricted. Because the definition of what constitutes a 'tangible contribution' is itself a normative community decision, the ISR does not encode a universal morality. It models objective interaction patterns to make social processes visible while preserving the possibility of disagreement, revision, and transformation. The ISR therefore does not encode a universal morality; it operates through an explicit and contestable "Vector of Values" defined by the community. This architecture structurally guards against the silent reproduction of existing hierarchies. Furthermore, the community's ability to periodically pull the tool back into Vorhandenheit to review and debate those very metrics becomes its own authentic political act. By explicitly leaving the essence of politics and interaction to humans, the architecture ensures that authentic social deeds can emerge.

6. Conclusion

Digital democracy fails fundamentally when it operates as a digital panopticon attempting to measure or capture the existential core. This approach commits a fatal ontological error by forcing Dasein into the calculable metrics of Vorhandenheit.

Conversely, computational governance succeeds when it functions merely as a reliable exoskeleton for microcommunities, acting as an invisible tool that resolves objective friction. By maintaining this strict architectural boundary — delegating the structural weight of Das Man to the machine — the system preserves human authenticity.

The future of digital democracy may not depend on making machines more human, but on designing machines that understand their own limits. The highest function of computation may be not to govern human beings, but to remove unnecessary friction so humans can govern themselves. In this sense, the Index of Social Responsibility is not a mechanism of automated governance, but an infrastructure for the reappearance of political agency.
