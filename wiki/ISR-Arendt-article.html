Towards a Computational Space of Appearance: Creating the Conditions for Inclusive Political Action at Post-Dunbar Scale

Sergii Ponomarchuk - April 2026

Abstract

This paper examines Hannah Arendt's diagnosis of modern political life and argues that the computational frameworks now emerging offer an historically unprecedented opportunity: not to recover a Space of Appearance that once existed, but to create, for the first time, the structural conditions for genuinely inclusive political action at scales that exceed the cognitive limits of unaided human sociality. We situate this argument in dialogue with the dominant contemporary critique of algorithmic systems - developed by Rosa, Rouvroy, Zuboff, and Srnicek - and contend that the pathologies these critics identify are features of a specific political-economic architecture, not of computational mediation as such. The Index of Social Responsibility (ISR) is examined as one worked example of a framework designed on contrary architectural principles: appearance over anonymity, deliberation over preemption, community-governed values over proprietary extraction, and individual accountability over role-diffusion. Drawing on Milgram's obedience experiments and Arendt's concept of the banality of evil, we argue that individual accountability is not merely an ethical preference but a structural prerequisite for political action. The paper concludes with an honest critical examination of what ISR does not yet resolve and what open research questions its design raises.

Keywords: political action, Hannah Arendt, Space of Appearance, algorithmic governmentality, augmented democracy, social responsibility, post-Dunbar scale, individual accountability

Introduction

In 1958, Hannah Arendt published The Human Condition, articulating a prescient critique of modern society's drift toward what she termed the triumph of animal laborans - the laboring animal absorbed in biological necessity - over genuine political action. Her central concern was not a technical failure of democratic institutions but a deeper civilizational shift: the displacement of the political realm by the social and the economic, and the consequent erosion of the Space of Appearance, the fragile domain in which human beings reveal themselves to one another through speech and deed.

Arendt's diagnosis has proved more durable than most of her contemporaries anticipated. The sixty years since its articulation have seen successive waves of technological and institutional change that have, in the main, confirmed rather than falsified her prognosis. Digital platforms have not restored the Space of Appearance; they have commercialized its simulation. Representative democracy has not recovered its legitimacy; it has continued its steady erosion. And the dominant intellectual response to these developments - a well-grounded critique of algorithmic governance, surveillance capitalism, and platform power - while diagnostically accurate, has largely stopped short of proposing structural alternatives.

This paper takes the Arendtian diagnosis seriously and asks a different question: what would it take to create, not merely restore, the conditions for genuine political action? Not to return to some historical model - the Athenian agora, the revolutionary council - that excluded the vast majority of human beings by design, but to build, for the first time, a Space of Appearance structurally open to the full demographic reality of modern communities. This is, in Arendt's own vocabulary, a question about natality: the human capacity to begin something genuinely new, to introduce a beginning that has no precedent.

We examine the Index of Social Responsibility (ISR) as one worked example of a computational framework designed with this ambition. A core claim of the paper is that this architectural focus on individual accountability responds directly to the line of thought running from Arendt's analysis of the "banality of evil" in Eichmann in Jerusalem to Milgram's obedience experiments, which together suggest that the diffusion of responsibility in modern institutions is not an ethical accident but a structural feature. ISR is not presented here as a proven solution but as a design proposal that illustrates what the structural conditions for inclusive political action at post-Dunbar scale might look like, and what the hardest questions are that any such proposal must answer.

The paper is organized in four parts. Part I develops the diagnostic tradition from Arendt through her contemporary inheritors. Part II presents the ISR framework as a counter-example to the dominant digital architecture. Part III addresses the strongest objections from the critical tradition directly. Part IV offers an honest assessment of what ISR does not resolve and what research remains open.

► Part I: The Diagnosis Extended - Arendt and Her Contemporary Inheritors

1.1 Arendt's Analytical Framework

Arendt's conceptual architecture in The Human Condition rests on a tripartite distinction within the vita activa: labor, the cyclical biological process of consumption and reproduction; work, the fabrication of durable objects that constitute a shared human world; and action, the only activity that occurs directly between persons, without the mediation of things, and the only sphere in which human beings can truly realize freedom and plurality. Action is the category Arendt identifies with politics in the fullest sense: it is the activity through which the who of a person is disclosed, through which new beginnings enter the world, and through which genuine power - as opposed to mere violence or administration - is generated and sustained.

The Space of Appearance is not a place but a condition: it comes into being whenever people gather to act and speak in concert, and it dissolves when they disperse. Its fragility is structural. Because it exists only in its actualization, it requires active, continuous renewal. What Arendt diagnosed in modern societies was the progressive destruction of the conditions under which this space can arise: the subordination of action to the cyclical logic of labor, the replacement of worldly durability with consumer impermanence, and the colonization of the political realm by economic administration. As Steven Klein (2014, p. 857) observes, for Arendt, "the danger is not the invasion of politics by economics, but rather the loss of the worldly, mediating institutions that allow economic matters to appear as objects of public concern."

A second concept central to this paper is Arendt's idea of natality: the human capacity to begin, to introduce something genuinely new into the world. For Arendt, natality is the ontological ground of political action: what distinguishes action from labor and work is precisely that it brings forth unprecedented beginnings and, in doing so, discloses who acts. Any institutional design that aims to eliminate unpredictability and initiative - that leaves no space for new questions, new actors, or new combinations of perspective - eliminates political action along with them. This is the lens through which the rest of the paper approaches computational mediation: the question is whether a framework, at post-Dunbar scale, a Space of Appearance in which new initiatives can still emerge and be attributed to the persons who begin them, rather than being absorbed into anonymous systems.

Part II takes up this question directly, arguing that computational systems could be understood not as a tool for optimizing a fixed order, but as an attempt to institutionalize new beginnings and non-delegable responsibility at scale.

1.2 The Scale Problem Arendt Did Not Theorize

There is a dimension of the political crisis Arendt diagnosed but did not fully theorize: the cognitive constraint on sociality at scale. The anthropologist Robin Dunbar (1992) demonstrated that the human neocortex imposes a practical limit of approximately 150 on the number of stable, trust-based social relationships an individual can maintain. Below this threshold - what has become known as Dunbar's number - informal reputation, mutual knowledge, and direct accountability function without institutional mediation. Communities at this scale are, in a meaningful sense, self-governing by default: everyone knows who contributed what, who can be trusted in which context, and who bears responsibility for what outcome.

The Space of Appearance in its historical instantiations - the Athenian agora, the revolutionary council, the medieval commune - operated at or near this scale. And crucially, it operated with radical exclusion: the agora excluded women, slaves, and foreigners, constituting roughly 85% of the Athenian population. The revolutionary councils Arendt admired were, in practice, assemblies of propertied, literate, politically active men. There is no historical precedent for a Space of Appearance that has been genuinely open to the full demographic complexity of a modern community, let alone a modern society.

This is not a criticism of Arendt but an extension of her diagnosis to a problem she could not fully anticipate. The political crisis of modernity is not only the displacement of action by labor - it is the structural impossibility of sustained informal political community above Dunbar-scale without either representative delegation (which hollows out action) or computational assistance (which introduces new risks). The question is not whether to use technological mediation at post-Dunbar scale; it is how to organize that mediation so that it extends rather than forecloses political action.

1.3 The Contemporary Critique: From Arendt to Algorithmic Governmentality

Four major currents of contemporary thought have extended and deepened Arendt's diagnosis in the digital age, constituting a coherent critical tradition that this paper takes seriously before proposing any response.

Hartmut Rosa: Acceleration and the Loss of Resonance. Rosa's (2013) theory of social acceleration identifies a structural temporal mismatch: democratic deliberation is inherently slow, requiring time for reflection, argument, and revision, while the pace of economic and technological change renders its outcomes obsolete before they can take effect. This "desynchronization" produces what Rosa (2019) calls alienation in its most general form: the inability to enter into genuine, responsive relations with the world. His concept of Resonance - the experience of being genuinely touched by and responding to the world - names what is absent from accelerated modernity. Critically, Rosa insists that resonance cannot be manufactured or optimized; it can only be enabled by creating the structural conditions in which it becomes possible (Unverfügbarkeit: the irreducible non-availability of resonance to deliberate production). This has direct implications for any computational governance framework: such a framework can, at best, create the structural conditions under which resonance and genuine political engagement become more likely; it cannot produce them.

Antoinette Rouvroy: Algorithmic Governmentality. Rouvroy's (2013, with Berns) concept of algorithmic governmentality describes a form of power that bypasses subjects as political agents entirely. Rather than addressing individuals through law, norms, or deliberation, algorithmic systems act on populations by processing behavioral data - "infra-individual fragments" such as clicks, dwell times, and purchasing patterns - to construct statistical profiles and modulate behavior preemptively. What is most significant about this form of power is precisely its invisibility: it operates below the threshold of political contestation, producing effects without making claims that can be challenged. Normativity is no longer articulated through public reasoning but embedded in predictive infrastructures. The result is what Rouvroy calls the "death of politics": governance reduced to optimization, eliminating the "power of irruption" - the capacity for the genuinely new and unexpected - that Arendt identified as the essence of political action.

Shoshana Zuboff: Surveillance Capitalism. Zuboff (2019) provides the economic-structural analysis that grounds Rouvroy's philosophical account. Surveillance capitalism is a novel market logic in which human experience is systematically converted into behavioral data, which is then processed to generate "behavioral futures" - predictions about what users will do next - that are sold to advertisers and others seeking to modify behavior at scale. What appears as participation (posting, liking, sharing) is structurally a form of extraction: users contribute data to systems that use it to influence them without meaningful consent or awareness. Zuboff's key insight is that this is not an accidental pathology of digital capitalism but its defining logic: the data that enables behavioral modification is the product being sold, and the human being generating it is the raw material.

Nick Srnicek: Platform Capitalism. Srnicek (2016) complements Zuboff with a structural economic analysis: digital platforms tend systemically toward monopoly through network effects (more users generate more data, enabling better services, attracting more users), creating winner-takes-all dynamics that concentrate unprecedented knowledge and decision-making power in the hands of a small number of private actors. This concentration is not incidentally but structurally anti-democratic: it produces radical asymmetries of information and power between platforms and users, and between platforms and public institutions, that make democratic oversight increasingly difficult.

Taken together, these four critiques constitute a powerful and well-grounded indictment of the dominant digital architecture. They converge on a shared diagnosis: existing computational systems tend to hollow out political agency, not extend it - not because computation is inherently incompatible with politics, but because the specific political-economic architecture within which current digital systems operate is designed around extraction, preemption, and opacity rather than deliberation, appearance, and accountability. The question the next parts of this paper address is whether a differently designed computational architecture is possible - and what it would require.

► Part II: Towards Creation - ISR as a Proposal for Inclusive Political Action

2.1 The Argument for Creation Rather than Recovery

The dominant framing of democratic reform - in political theory and in practice - is restorative: recover what was lost, return to genuine participation, rebuild the public sphere. This framing contains a historical error. The Space of Appearance, in any of its historical instantiations, was never genuinely inclusive. The Athenian agora, which Arendt treats as a touchstone, excluded approximately 85% of the city's population by formal legal design. The revolutionary councils she admired in On Revolution (1963) were assemblies of propertied men. Medieval communes, guild assemblies, and early modern civic bodies all operated on exclusion as a structural premise, not a contingent oversight.

If this is correct, the appropriate framework is not restoration but creation. What computational infrastructure makes possible, for the first time in human history, is a Space of Appearance that does not depend on geographic co-location, shared ethnicity, property ownership, or gender for its constitution. It can include, in principle, anyone who is genuinely affected by a given issue - regardless of where they live, what they own, or what social categories they inhabit. This is not a modest incremental reform of democratic institutions; it is a qualitatively new possibility that has no historical precedent.

In Arendt's own vocabulary, this is a question of natality: the introduction into the world of something genuinely new. The ISR framework is examined here not as a restoration of the council system but as a proposal for what the council system's structural logic - direct participation, issue-specific assembly, non-delegable accountability - might look like when extended, for the first time, to the full complexity of modern communities at post-Dunbar scale.

2.2 The Dunbar Constraint and the Role of Computational Mediation

Dunbar's (1992) neocortex constraint on group size is not merely an interesting anthropological observation. It is a structural fact about the conditions under which informal political community is possible. Below approximately 150 members, a community can sustain the mutual knowledge, informal reputation, and direct accountability that make genuine political action possible without institutional mediation. Everyone knows who contributed what, whose judgment to trust on which questions, and who bears responsibility for which outcomes. The Space of Appearance at this scale arises, in a sense, naturally - as a byproduct of shared life.

Above this threshold, the informal mechanisms break down. Communities larger than Dunbar's number require some form of institutional mediation to sustain accountability, recognize contribution, and ensure that relevant voices reach collective decision-making. The historical answer has been representative delegation - which Arendt correctly identified as purchasing scale at the cost of genuine political action. The contemporary digital answer has been platform aggregation - which Rouvroy, Zuboff, and Srnicek correctly identify as purchasing reach at the cost of genuine agency.

ISR proposes a third architecture: computational mediation that restores the functional properties of Dunbar-scale community - mutual accountability, contribution recognition, contextual relevance - at post-Dunbar scale, without requiring geographic co-location and without the exclusions that characterized all historical instances of such community. This is the structural claim at the heart of the ISR proposal.

2.3 The ISR Framework: Core Architecture

Figure 1: Comparison of Traditional Representative Democracy and ISR Model

The ISR framework is designed around a single organizing principle: influence on a collective decision should reflect genuine stake in its outcome, not formal status, not seniority, not numerical majority. To implement this principle computationally, ISR assembles what it calls a Microcommunity for each decision: a dynamic, issue-specific group constituted by all members who are genuinely affected by the question at hand.

Microcommunity formation follows the All-Affected Principle from democratic theory (Ober, 2008): those whose interests are materially at stake in a decision have a legitimate claim to participate in it. Membership is not elected, not delegated, and not permanent. It is determined by the nature of the problem and dissolves when the problem is resolved. This preserves the Arendtian understanding of political action as oriented toward specific, concrete situations rather than abstract administration, and it mirrors the structural logic of Arendt's admired council system - direct participation, issue-specific assembly, non-delegable presence - without the historical exclusions that characterized actual councils.

Figure 2: Microcommunity Lifecycle - Dynamic Problem-Oriented Formation

Within each Microcommunity, each member's influence weight is computed from three components, the relative importance of which is determined by the issue context.

  • Relevance - how directly this member is affected by the specific issue at hand. Relevance is not binary: it is a continuous coefficient reflecting proximity, geographic connection, and social-graph relationship to the parties involved. Even belonging to a Microcommunity is a matter of degree, not a threshold.
  • Expertise - what relevant knowledge or verifiable track record this member brings to the specific question. This addresses Arendt's concern that modern political life fails to distinguish between opinion and knowledge. It finds empirical-historical grounding in Ober's (2008) study of Athenian democracy, which demonstrated that the polis's capacity to harness distributed citizen knowledge was a primary source of its political and economic success.
  • Contribution - this member's history of actions within the community, evaluated against the community's own stated values. This is the component that restores the Arendtian connection between rights and responsibility: influence is proportional not to what you claim but to what you have demonstrably done. It is also the component most directly responsive to the values-based framework of the community: the same action may be evaluated differently by different Microcommunities, because each community defines its own Matrix of Values.

Further technical documentation, including formal definitions, examples, and implementation notes, is maintained on the ISR site.

2.4 The Matrix of Values: Community-Generated Normative Ground

A central design feature of ISR is that no values are preset from above. Each member of a community declares their own values across a structured set of dimensions. The community's shared Matrix of Values for any given Microcommunity emerges from the weighted aggregation of individual declarations. This ensures that the normative framework against which actions are evaluated is generated by the community itself, not imposed by the system's designers.

This design addresses a fundamental objection to computational governance: that any algorithmic system encodes the values of its designers and imposes them on its users under the guise of neutrality. ISR's response is structural rather than merely procedural: the representational scaffold (the set of dimensions across which values are declared) is a tool provided by the system, but the values themselves - and the relative weights assigned to each dimension - are entirely community-determined. The scaffold provides a common notation; the community composes the score. Individual values do not change merely because someone enters a new Microcommunity; they change only when the member revises them. This preserves genuine plurality: the community's shared framework is the aggregate of genuinely distinct individual positions, not a homogenized consensus.

The current value structure (233-dimension vector including grounding and context shift markers) represents version 1.0 of an open research problem. Sustained work in sociology, moral philosophy, and computational social science will be necessary to develop it further. Future versions may differ substantially as that research matures.

2.5 Individual Responsibility and the Architecture of Accountability

The ISR framework's insistence on individual, non-delegable accountability is not a design preference. It is a response to one of the most disturbing findings of twentieth-century political and social science: that ordinary, functional people - neither sadistic nor ideologically fanatical - are capable of participating in atrocity when institutional structures distribute and diffuse responsibility across a collective.

Hannah Arendt's report on the trial of Adolf Eichmann - Eichmann in Jerusalem: A Report on the Banality of Evil (1963) - produced the concept that has become her most enduring contribution: the banality of evil. Eichmann was not a monster. He was a competent, career-oriented bureaucrat who organized the logistics of mass deportation without any evident personal animus toward his victims. Arendt's terrifying conclusion was that he "never realized what he was doing" - not because he lacked intelligence, but because he had surrendered the faculty of independent moral judgment to institutional hierarchy. His repeated formula - "I was just following orders," "I was doing my job," "the decision came from above" - exemplifies what Arendt identified as the institutional architecture of moral evasion.

Stanley Milgram's obedience experiments (Milgram, 1974) provided the social-psychological evidence for what Arendt had diagnosed philosophically. In Milgram's most famous procedure, 65% of ordinary adults administered what they believed were dangerous electric shocks to strangers, continuing as the apparent victims cried out in pain, simply because an authority figure instructed them to. The defining variable was not the subjects' personalities but the structure of the situation: an authority who claimed responsibility, gradual escalation of commitment, and diffusion of moral agency across a chain in which the majority of individuals didn't feel they were making the decisive choice. When the authority was absent or the subject in direct physical proximity to the victim, compliance dropped sharply. Responsibility is not merely an inner state - it is a structural condition that institutional design either enables or undermines.

Both Arendt and Milgram converge on the same structural conclusion: the architecture of collective institutions allow individual actors feel that their choices are not really their own. When that happens, the capacity for genuinely moral action - what Arendt called thinking, the inner dialogue she associated directly with the refusal to participate in evil - is suppressed by the institution rather than exercised within it.

ISR is designed around the opposite structural principle. Every action is attributed to the person who took it - not to the role, the organization, the majority that sanctioned it, or the superior who authorized it. "I was following orders" and "the community decided" are not recognized categories in ISR's accountability model. The evasion "the algorithm decided" is structurally refused in exactly the same way: systems are made and maintained by people who are responsible for what those systems do. Every score is traceable to specific decisions, every parameter is the explicit, contestable choice of identifiable community members.

At the same time, the institutional tendency to “functionalize” persons - to organize work as standardized, tightly specified roles - intersects with recent global analyses of AI and employment in a way that sharpens, rather than softens, the responsibility problem. The IMF, ILO, and OECD now converge on the finding that the jobs most exposed to generative AI in the near term are precisely those structured as template-based, digitally mediated functions - above all clerical and administrative work, but also many routine professional and technical roles whose content can be decomposed into repeatable informational tasks (IMF 2024; ILO 2025; OECD 2024). When human beings accept self-understandings that mirror this organization - “I am just my function” - they render their contribution structurally more substitutable by systems designed to perform those functions more cheaply and reliably. Yet the capacity at stake in Arendt's analysis of Eichmann and in Milgram's subjects - the refusal or acceptance of responsibility for what one is doing - is not a functional capacity. It belongs to persons, not to tools.

ISR's accountability architecture is designed as a direct counter to both forms of evasion: the bureaucratic “I was just doing my job” and the technological “the algorithm decided.” It does not presuppose a prior culture of civic virtue; it simply requires that every action within the system be attributable to an identifiable person, and that each community be willing to declare the values against which it wants those actions evaluated. From the moment such a framework is adopted, Contribution becomes a cumulative parameter of political standing: a concrete history of accountable actions that immediately begins to shape who has influence over shared decisions. The point is not to wait for perfect conditions and then introduce responsibility; it is to begin from the conditions we have and to make patterns of responsibility and irresponsibility structurally visible from the start.

This is not a claim that ISR eliminates the risk of moral diffusion. Any sufficiently complex system can create conditions in which no individual feels the full weight of a collective decision. What ISR does is make that diffusion structurally harder, and make the evasion of personal responsibility structurally visible.

2.6 Transparency, Appearance, and the Absence of Anonymity

Arendt's Space of Appearance requires precisely what its name indicates: individuals must appear before one another, disclosing who they are through their words and deeds in the presence of others. Anonymity is structurally incompatible with political action in this sense - not because anonymous speech has no value, but because political action in the Arendtian sense requires the agent to take responsibility for their words and deeds in a shared world.

ISR implements this requirement structurally: every action in the system is associated with a verified identity. This does not mean universal transparency of all information; the Adaptable Authorization system governs who can see what, calibrated to the context of the accessor. Privacy and accountability are balanced by design, not traded off. What is eliminated is not privacy but the specific form of irresponsibility that anonymity enables: saying what one does not mean, committing to what one does not intend, and disappearing from the consequences of one's political acts.

2.7 AI as Political Infrastructure, Not Political Authority

A clarification that runs throughout this paper deserves to be made explicit: ISR does not propose that AI should govern communities. It proposes that specific computational capabilities - the ability to process large sets of declared values, track behavioral histories, compute contextual relevance, and surface the results transparently - can restore, at post-Dunbar scale, the conditions for genuine human deliberation that cannot be sustained without technological assistance above a certain scale of community.

The distinction matters precisely because it is the distinction between the systems Rouvroy, Zuboff, and Srnicek criticize and what ISR proposes. A system in which an algorithm makes decisions about who has power is a technocracy. A system in which an algorithm computes, transparently, the influence weight that a community's own stated values would assign to each of its members for each specific issue - and in which every parameter of that computation is inspectable, contestable, and revisable by the community itself - is something structurally different: a tool in service of political action, not a substitute for it.

Rosa's concept of resonance is useful here. ISR cannot manufacture the experience of genuine political engagement; resonance, as Rosa insists, is unverfügbar - structurally unavailable to deliberate production. What ISR can do is create the structural conditions that make resonance more likely: ensuring that voices are heard in proportion to genuine stake, that contributions are recognized, that accountability is real. These are preconditions for resonance, not its guarantee.

► Part III: The Critical Test - Answering the Objections

The critical tradition surveyed in Part I deserves direct engagement, not merely acknowledgment. Each of the four major objections to computational governance frameworks has a specific structural form, and ISR either addresses that structural form or must honestly acknowledge where it does not.

3.1 The Rouvroy Objection: Does ISR Bypass Deliberation?

The objection: Algorithmic governmentality acts on populations by processing behavioral data preemptively, bypassing subjects as deliberating agents. Normativity is embedded in predictive infrastructure rather than articulated through public reasoning. The "death of politics" occurs when governance operates below the threshold of contestation.

The systems Rouvroy (2013) describes share three structural features: they act on infra-individual fragments (behavioral data generated passively); they operate preemptively (shaping possible actions before deliberation occurs); and they are invisible (producing effects without making contestable claims). ISR is architecturally the opposite on all three dimensions.

First, ISR acts not on passive behavioral fragments but on explicitly assumed responsibility. The data that enters the system is generated through political engagement - declared values, logged actions, deliberative participation - not captured from passive behavior. The user is not a data source; they are a participant who chooses what to contribute.

Second, ISR does not operate preemptively. It does not shape possible actions before deliberation occurs; it weights personal decision influence on options produced during deliberation. The sequence is: issue arises → Microcommunity assembles → personal influence weights are determined → members deliberate → members formulate decision options → members make decision by weighted voting. The algorithm serves the deliberative process; it does not replace it.

Third, ISR is designed for maximum transparency, not invisibility. Every component of every influence score is inspectable by the person it concerns. Every parameter is contestable and community-revisable. The system makes explicit claims - this action was evaluated this way, against these community-defined criteria - that can be publicly challenged. This is the structural condition for political contestation, not its elimination.

3.2 The Zuboff Objection: Is ISR a Form of Extraction?

The objection: Surveillance capitalism converts human experience into behavioral data for behavioral modification without meaningful consent. What appears as participation is structurally extraction. The "Big Other" accumulates knowledge and power that operates outside democratic oversight.

Zuboff's (2019) critique targets a specific economic logic: the monetization of behavioral prediction. Data is extracted from users without their understanding, processed to generate behavioral futures, and sold to third parties seeking to modify behavior for profit. The user is both the raw material and the target of the system.

ISR inverts this logic entirely. There is no third-party behavioral market in ISR's design. The data generated by participation - declared values, contribution history, deliberative actions - belongs to the community and to the individual member. It is used exclusively to compute influence weights and provide that community's deliberative processes. There is no "Big Other" external to the community that accumulates and monetizes this data. The community is the owner and the beneficiary of the information its members generate through participation.

The deeper point concerns what Zuboff calls "behavioral modification." ISR does not attempt to modify behavior through invisible prediction. It makes the criteria for influence explicit and public, and relies on the rational self-interest of participants who understand those criteria to motivate accountability. If members know that their actions are evaluated against the community's values, and that consistent accountability increases their influence in future deliberations, they have a transparent, publicly articulable reason to act accountably. This is not manipulation; it is the classical mechanism of reputation in small communities, made operational at larger scale.

3.3 The Srnicek Objection: Does ISR Reproduce Platform Power?

The objection: Digital platforms tend toward monopoly through network effects, concentrating knowledge and decision-making power. Even participatory-seeming systems may generate asymmetries that are structurally anti-democratic.

Srnicek's (2016) concern is structural: the economic logic of platforms generates monopolistic concentration regardless of the intentions of their designers. Network effects create winner-takes-all dynamics; data advantages compound; and the result is a small number of private actors with unprecedented power over the information environment.

ISR's architecture addresses this through three design principles. First, the system is designed as open-source infrastructure: the algorithm is publicly auditable, not proprietary. Second, the governance of the system's parameters is community-controlled: no external actor can modify the weights or criteria against which community members are evaluated without community consent. Third, the data generated within each community remains within that community: there is no data aggregation across communities that would generate platform-scale power concentrations.

This does not mean ISR is immune to capture or monopolization. The risk is real and must be addressed through institutional design: federated architecture (so that no single actor controls the infrastructure), community ownership of data, and open-source auditability of all algorithmic components. These are design requirements, not achieved properties of the current system, and they constitute an active research and development agenda.

3.4 The Rosa Objection: Can a Computational System Enable Resonance?

The objection: Social acceleration produces alienation by making genuine responsive relations with the world increasingly impossible. Resonance - the experience of being genuinely touched by and responding to the world - cannot be manufactured, optimized, or produced by design. Any system that claims to generate resonance is structurally self-defeating.

This objection is accepted on its own terms. ISR does not claim to manufacture resonance or to guarantee the experience of genuine political engagement. Rosa's Unverfügbarkeit - the irreducible non-availability of resonance to deliberate production - is taken as a firm constraint on what any institutional or computational design can achieve.

What ISR claims is more modest: that it can create the structural conditions under which resonance becomes more likely, by ensuring that political participation is connected to real stakes and real consequences. When a community member's influence on a decision reflects their genuine stake in its outcome, when their contributions are recognized and their accountability is real, when they appear before their community as a named, responsible agent rather than an anonymous signal - these are the structural prerequisites for the kind of genuine engagement that Rosa identifies as resonance. The experience itself cannot be guaranteed. The conditions for its possibility can be deliberately constructed or destroyed.

The acceleration objection also points to a genuine limit of ISR's design: the system requires time for genuine deliberation, and the pressures of social acceleration work against this. An ISR-structured community can resist acceleration within its own deliberative processes; it cannot insulate itself from the broader temporal demands of the economic environment in which it operates. This is acknowledged as a real limitation.

3.5 The General Objection: Is Technology Necessarily Dehumanizing?

Underlying the specific objections above is a more general concern: that the introduction of computational mediation into political life is inherently dehumanizing, that any system that quantifies human contribution or weights human influence has already reduced political life to administration. This concern has a long history, from Luddite resistance to industrial machinery through contemporary AI-phobia, and it deserves a respectful and direct response.

The error in this general objection is the conflation of a tool with its use. Writing is a tool for extending human communication beyond the limits of face-to-face speech; it has been used to compose both Hamlet and propaganda. Printing is a tool for multiplying texts; it enabled both the Reformation and the Inquisition's censorship apparatus. The internet is a tool for connecting human beings at global scale; it has enabled both unprecedented cooperation and unprecedented manipulation. In each case, the tool is not the pathology. The pathology is the specific political-economic architecture within which the tool is deployed and the specific purposes it is organized to serve.

Computation is a tool of precisely this kind. The systems Rouvroy, Zuboff, and Srnicek criticize are not evidence that computation is inherently incompatible with political life; they are evidence that computation organized around extraction, preemption, and opacity is incompatible with political life. The question for system designers is not "whether to use computation" but "what architecture, what governance, what transparency requirements, and what accountability mechanisms make computational mediation serve rather than displace human agency?" ISR is one attempt to answer that question. It may not be the right answer, but the question itself is unavoidable: at post-Dunbar scale, without some form of computational mediation, the structural conditions for inclusive political action do not exist. The choice is not between computation and politics but between different computational architectures and the political conditions they create.

► Part IV: Open Questions - What ISR Does Not Resolve

The argument of this paper would be incomplete without an honest accounting of what ISR does not yet resolve. The following represent genuine open questions, not rhetorical concessions.

4.1 Structural and Philosophical Challenges

The scale paradox. ISR is designed to extend Dunbar-scale political community to larger organizations. But the systems of greatest political urgency - climate governance, pandemic response, AI regulation, nuclear risk management - operate at genuinely civilizational scales, affecting billions of people whose stake in collective decisions is real but whose connection to any specific microcommunity is diffuse. The All-Affected Principle, when applied to civilizational-scale issues, generates a Microcommunity of the entire human species. How ISR's architecture handles this extension is an open question that cannot be answered by design principles alone; it requires empirical testing at successively larger scales.

The meritocracy tension. ISR's Expertise and Contribution components introduce influence differentials that are justified by reference to relevant knowledge and demonstrated accountability. Arendt herself was suspicious of any system that reduced political standing to measurable performance - this approaches what she criticized as the "rise of the social," where conformity to external standards displaces genuine political plurality. ISR's structural response - that the criteria are context-specific and community-defined rather than externally imposed - is conceptually defensible but empirically untested. Whether communities actually define their criteria in ways that prevent meritocratic calcification into new forms of hierarchy is an empirical question that only longitudinal study of deployed systems can answer.

The Arendt tension. Arendt's own preferred model - the council system - was explicitly non-algorithmic. Political life, for Arendt, was inseparable from the direct, embodied, unmediated encounter of distinct persons. ISR uses algorithmic mediation. This is a genuine departure from her prescription, even while extending her diagnosis. ISR is an attempt to answer the problem Arendt identified using means she did not theorize; whether those means are adequate to the problem, or whether they introduce new pathologies she would have recognized, is a question that honest scholarship must hold open.

The values-measurement problem. The ISR value vectorization system - the structured representation of member values across defined dimensions - is version 1.0 of an open research problem in computational social science. Any such structure carries the implicit assumptions of its designers. Sustained empirical and theoretical research is required before strong claims about the adequacy of any specific value representation can be made.

4.2 Implementation and Social Challenges

Elite capture. Any governance system that assigns differential influence creates incentives for those with resources and organizational capacity to capture the high-influence positions. In ISR, this risk manifests as the possibility of coordinated Contribution manipulation: networks of actors who systematically endorse each other's actions to inflate mutual scores. The system's technical safeguards (anomaly detection, cross-community consistency requirements) reduce but do not eliminate this risk. It requires both technical and governance solutions, and the governance solutions require exactly the kind of active community engagement that elite capture tends to suppress.

The exclusion of the disengaged. ISR's Contribution component rewards active participation and penalizes inactivity. This is philosophically defensible - political influence earned through demonstrated engagement - but it raises a practical equity concern: the communities most in need of better governance are often those whose members are least positioned to invest time and energy in active participation (due to precarious employment, caregiving responsibilities, language barriers, or accumulated civic disillusionment). A framework that strengthens the voices of the already-engaged may reproduce the inequalities it aims to displace in a new form.

Cultural inertia and the habituation requirement. Decades of political disengagement, eroded civic education, and normalized irresponsibility create significant obstacles to adoption. ISR requires communities willing to take active ownership of their governance - a disposition that cannot be assumed as a starting condition but must be cultivated over time. The framework is explicitly designed with this cultivation in mind, by linking influence to demonstrated responsibility, but whether this evolutionary mechanism is strong enough to shift entrenched habits of passivity is an open empirical question rather than an achieved fact.

Technological dependence and federated risk. The system's reliance on AI infrastructure creates forms of potential dependence and vulnerability that have no precedent in Arendt's framework. Open-source transparency, federated architecture, and community ownership of the computational layer are necessary but not sufficient: they require ongoing technical maintenance, governance capacity, and protection against state or corporate capture that cannot be guaranteed by design principles alone.

4.3 Open Research Questions

The following questions represent the minimal research agenda that must be addressed before strong empirical claims about ISR's political effects can be made:

  1. Do communities using ISR actually experience greater political engagement, accountability, and decision legitimacy than comparable communities using traditional governance mechanisms? This requires controlled longitudinal study with appropriate comparison groups.
  2. Does the Contribution component, in practice, reward genuine accountability or reward the performance of accountability to an audience that includes the scoring system? The distinction between virtue and its simulation is the oldest problem in moral philosophy; ISR must demonstrate, not assume, that its structural mechanisms address it.
  3. Do value structures defined by communities remain genuinely plural, or do they converge toward consensus under the pressure of influence-weighting? Genuine plurality may require active institutional protection, not just structural permission.
  4. How does ISR perform under adversarial conditions - when a significant minority of participants actively attempt to manipulate scores, capture Microcommunity formation, or undermine the legitimacy of deliberative outcomes? Resilience under adversarial conditions is a necessary, not optional, design requirement.
  5. What is the appropriate relationship between ISR-structured community governance and formal legal and political institutions? ISR governs influence in deliberative processes; it does not replace law, enforce decisions, or provide due process guarantees. The interface between ISR governance and the broader legal framework it operates within requires explicit design.

► Conclusion

This paper has argued for a shift in the framing of democratic reform: from recovery to creation. The Space of Appearance - in any of its historical instantiations - has never been genuinely inclusive. Computational infrastructure makes it structurally possible, for the first time, to create the conditions for political action that is not premised on exclusion. This is not a modest reform; it is an application of Arendt's own concept of natality - the introduction of genuinely new beginnings into the world - to the project of political institution-building.

The dominant critical tradition surveyed in Part I - Rouvroy's algorithmic governmentality, Zuboff's surveillance capitalism, Srnicek's platform capitalism, Rosa's acceleration theory - constitutes a powerful and accurate diagnosis of what the prevailing digital architecture does to political life. This paper takes that diagnosis seriously. Its response is not to defend computation against critique but to distinguish the pathological political-economic architecture that these critics correctly identify from the structural possibilities of computation as such. The systems they critique share three features: preemption over deliberation, extraction over participation, and invisibility over contestability. ISR is designed against all three.

The objection that any computational mediation of political life is inherently dehumanizing confuses a tool with its use. At post-Dunbar scale, the structural conditions for informal political community do not exist without mediation of some kind. The question is not whether to mediate but how. ISR proposes that mediation organized around transparency, individual accountability, community-governed values, and appearance - in Arendt's sense of the term - is structurally different from mediation organized around extraction, preemption, and opacity. Whether it is different enough, and whether the differences are sustainable under real-world pressures, are empirical questions that require longitudinal study of deployed systems.

The honest conclusion is that ISR is a proposal, not a proven system. It is one worked example of what an architecture designed to extend rather than foreclose political action might look like. Its value, at this stage, lies not in having resolved the problems it addresses but in having made them more precisely articulable - and in having demonstrated that the design space for computational governance frameworks is considerably larger, and more politically interesting, than the dominant critical literature has assumed.
