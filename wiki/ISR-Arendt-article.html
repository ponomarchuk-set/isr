Digital Democracy and the Recovery of Political Action: The Index of Social Responsibility as a Response to Arendt's Critique of Modern Depoliticization

by Sergii Ponomarchuk, 25.09.2025

Abstract

This paper examines Hannah Arendt's critique of modern society's depoliticization—the displacement of authentic political action by economic administration and world alienation—and proposes the Index of Social Responsibility (ISR) as a computational framework addressing this crisis. Where representative democracy reduces plural political agents to abstract voting units, and technocratic systems replace human judgment with expert administration, ISR implements a weighted decision-making approach grounded in three Arendtian principles: expertise (distinguishing knowledge from mere opinion), responsibility (connecting rights to accountability for one's actions), and contextual relevance (ensuring stakeholders in specific issues retain meaningful voice). Through microcommunities dynamically organized around concrete problems rather than pre-determined institutional hierarchies, ISR preserves the "space of appearance" that Arendt identified as essential to political action—where individuals reveal themselves as unique agents capable of initiating the unexpected. The framework positions AI as political infrastructure serving community-determined values rather than imposing technocratic logic, thereby recovering what Arendt called "power"—the capacity that emerges when people act in concert for shared worldly concerns. We argue that computational design need not reduce politics to administration; instead, it can restore conditions for authentic political plurality and the human freedom Arendt sought to preserve.

Keywords: political action, digital democracy, computational social science, Hannah Arendt, artificial intelligence, social responsibility

Introduction

In 1958, Hannah Arendt published "The Human Condition," articulating a prescient critique of modern society's drift toward what she termed the triumph of "animal laborans" over "political action". Her central concern was that modern society increasingly prioritizes economic consumption and welfare administration over genuine political action, leading to world alienation - the loss of an intersubjectively constituted world of experience and action, that enables authentic plurality. As Steven Klein observes, for Arendt, "the danger is not the invasion of politics by economics, but rather the loss of the worldly, mediating institutions that allow economic matters to appear as objects of public concern" (2014, p.487).

This paper argues that while digital technologies have exacerbated many of the problems Arendt identified, they also offer unprecedented opportunities to restore authentic political action. We propose the Index of Social Responsibility (ISR) as an approach that could address Arendt's fundamental concerns while leveraging AI capabilities to transcend the limitations of both traditional democracy and technocratic governance.

Part I: The Contemporary Relevance of Arendt's Critique

The Deepening Crisis of Political Action

Arendt's diagnosis of modern society's fundamental problem remains strikingly relevant. She identified three interconnected processes that undermine authentic political life: the "rise of the social", "world alienation", and the triumph of "animal laborans" over political agency. Each has been dramatically amplified by digital technologies.

The Digital Rise of the Social: Arendt's concept of the "rise of the social" - the blurring of distinctions between private and public realms, where market economy and social concerns dominate both spheres - finds new expression in social media platforms. As recent research demonstrates, these platforms transform political discourse by "quantifying communication through likes, shares, and followers, shifting substantive ends toward social status achievement" rather than genuine political deliberation (American Philosophical Association, 2025). This creates what scholars term "conformist conceptualization of human speech and action" that undermines authentic political plurality.

Accelerated World Alienation: Rosa's acceleration theory provides crucial insight into how digital technologies intensify Arendt's concept of world alienation. Hartmut Rosa’s diagnosis of social acceleration describes a pervasive modern dynamic driven by the pursuit of optimization and control. The ISR seeks to solve the crisis of alienation by creating conditions for increased personal responsibility and engagement. However, it must explicitly address Rosa's warning that resonance (the antidote to alienation) is inherently uncontrollable (Unverfügbarkeit). The ISR should therefore be framed as a system that increases reachability and accountability in governance, thus establishing a prerequisite for resonance in the sphere of Work, without claiming to manufacture resonance itself.

The Digitization of Animal Laborans: Contemporary platform capitalism represents the ultimate triumph of what Arendt termed "animal laborans" - humans reduced to laboring animals focused on biological reproduction and consumption. Maurizio Lazzarato's analysis of "machinic enslavement" reveals how digital platforms "progressively reduce the number of "recalcitrant and unreliable' human operators and multiply the more reliable "mechanical, electronic, and chemical' ones" (2014, p. 33). Platform workers exemplify this process, as they "labor on dedicated gig platforms, discuss through speech and action on social media platforms, and simultaneously work by contributing publicly available written traces" that feed AI systems without genuine political agency.

The Crisis of Representative Democracy

Current democratic systems exhibit precisely the problems Arendt identified with the delegation of political responsibility. As our analysis reveals, a person in the global world has a huge complex of representatives (both formal and informal) for every aspect of life because individuals belong to multiple social groups in different aspects. This creates a situation where any representative can't completely account for every group member's interests and values - it is always an average position corrected with human and professional characteristics of the representative.

One result we observe today is that many citizens feel increasingly disconnected from political decisions - they don't believe they can influence them. The word "responsibility" has acquired negative connotations, associated with limitation rather than empowerment, while "freedom" becomes reduced to freedom of consumption and entertainment.

This represents precisely the world alienation Arendt feared - a condition where humans lose connection to the durable, shared world that provides stability and enables authentic political action. Contemporary research confirms this diagnosis: "social media may reflect just "a form of world-alienation in interrelated ways'" where "the character of the world as a set of facts and objects agreed upon and recognized by our common senses" becomes increasingly impaired.

Part II: The Index of Social Responsibility as Computational Political Action

Theoretical Foundation: Beyond Binary Democracy



Figure 1: Comparison of Traditional Representative Democracy and ISR Model


The ISR framework addresses Arendt's concerns by creating what we term "computational political action" - a system that preserves human agency and plurality while leveraging AI capabilities to overcome the structural limitations of representative democracy. Unlike traditional "one-person-one-vote" systems that Arendt criticized for reducing politics to mere aggregation of preferences, ISR implements what can be called "weighted plurality" - a kind of voting coefficient of a certain person about a certain issue. The ISR system operates on the premise of universal legal equality and equal political potential among citizens. It uses weighted voting to reflect the unavoidable inequality of epistemic competence and contribution in administering shared concerns. This framework seeks to restore legitimacy by creating a direct connection between citizen accountability and decision influence (the Work realm), which representative systems have broken. In a specific case people have different levels of expertise, different skills, different history of interaction with society and different contextual connection to the case - that is why their influence on the issue should be different.

Microcommunities and the Recovery of Political Space

The ISR model proposes "microcommunities" as the fundamental unit of political organization, representing a technological solution to what Arendt identified as the core problem of modern politics: the loss of spaces where genuine plurality can emerge and be sustained.



Figure 2: Microcommunity Lifecycle: Dynamic Problem-Oriented Formation


Unlike traditional representative structures, microcommunities automatically gather people among registered users around specified issues to make group decisions about the future of the group in the context of the issue. They are dynamic rather than static, changing their members list and their weight according to the context changing, and after the problem is solved, microcommunity is automatically dismissed.

This addresses several key political concerns:

Temporal Responsiveness: Unlike bureaucratic structures that persist regardless of their relevance, microcommunities exist only as long as the issues they address require collective attention. This restores the Arendtian understanding of political action as oriented toward specific, concrete situations rather than abstract administration.

Direct Participation: Each microcommunity member gets rights and responsibility to influence the microcommunity decisions directly, without usual representative levels. Contextual Relevance: The AI-driven formation of microcommunities ensures that gathering occurs automatically with AI tools to count the wide context of the problem's scope and figure out the relative people. This technological mediation serves human political capacity rather than replacing it.

    • How Matrix of Values Is Used:

    1. Baseline Reference: The aggregated values serve as the community's shared baseline for what matters in this issue
    2. Influence Weighting: When calculating ISR influence scores (Expertise, Contribution, Relevance), the community's Matrix of Values determines Contribution, providing a badrock of actions assessment
    3. Holistic Assessment: Rather than imposing external criteria, the framework reflects what this specific community actually values for this specific issue
    4. Accountability: Members see their collective values reflected in decision-making processes, ensuring decisions align with community priorities

    Key Principle: Individual values don't change just because someone joins a microcommunity — only clarified through community context. The Matrix ensures pluralism by averaging diverse perspectives rather than erasing them.


    Figure 5: Matrix of Values: Individual to Community Aggregation


    Matrix of Values (Automated Legislature): It is a kind of legislature system in the ISR framework. Every user fills out its own life (economical, political, cultural, etc.) values. These values don't change because of the new microcommunity belonging - only if the member changes or clarifies his own life values. After gathering microcommunity around some problem, its "Matrix of Values" appears by averaging all its members' individual values concerning the problem. So we got an actual, inclusive, pluralistic basement to understand the microcommunity needs and values which can be used for all further ISR estimations.

    The next three elements make up the ISR as constituent parts.

    Key Principle of Weighted Plurality

    Unlike traditional "one-person-one-vote" systems, each member's influence coefficient varies based on:
    ▪ Their contextual connection to the issue
    ▪ Their proven track record of contribution
    ▪ Their actual expertise in the issue domain
    AI determine the weight (w₁, w₂, w₃) through the issue context.


    Figure 6: ISR Influence Score Calculation Model


    Expertise (Personal Competence): This component addresses Arendt's concern that modern society fails to distinguish between opinion and knowledge. By weighing decisions based on relevant skills, experience, or credentials that the community values, ISR ensures that those with greater expertise in specific domains have proportionally greater influence on decisions within those domains.

    Contribution (Responsibility History): This addresses Arendt's insight that political action requires accountability and continuity over time. Contribution represents a running tally of the member's contributions and feedback that reflects an individual's history of responsible engagement with community concerns. And ISR assumes that people who, according to the microcommunity's Matrix of Values, contributed more, have more rights to influence the microcommunity. This restores the connection between rights and responsibilities that Arendt saw as essential to authentic political life.

    Relevance (Situational and Contextual Connection): This component (which follows from the microcommunity gathering principle) ensures that influence is distributed based on actual stake and involvement in specific issues. As our framework explains, context-specific factors, like stake or activity in the current topic ensure that only relevant voices carry extra weight on a given decision. So even belonging to a microcommunity is not a binary. It is a coefficient that shows how close a person is to the microcommunity's issue. And the closer it is - the bigger impact the person has. This preserves Arendtian plurality by ensuring that different perspectives are heard and weighted appropriately based on their relevance to specific contexts.

    Transparency and Accountability

    The system requires the absence of anonymity and logs all actions. It means that every user has only one account and it is confirmed with documents and identification mechanisms and any action in the ISR framework can't be done anonymously. This does not mean that anyone can see all information about a user; rather, the level and scope of access depend on the person's social role and other factors. That ensures privacy on one hand and minding of responsibility on the other hand.

    Today's excessive worry about anonymity concerns is a part of our political legacy. The fear of being punished for stating a different point of view freely provokes hypocrisy on all social levels - a trauma stemming from the totalitarian aspects of modern life that the ISR framework aims to combat. This aligns with Arendt's insight that authentic political action requires the courage to appear before others and take responsibility for one's words and deeds. As she argued, politics requires the "space of appearance" where individuals reveal who they are through their actions in the presence of others.

    AI as a Tool of Political Infrastructure

    Crucially, the ISR framework positions AI not as a replacement for human political judgment but as infrastructure that enables more authentic political participation. The AI systems calculate a dynamic influence score for each member – based on their social contributions, personal competencies, and context relevance but the criteria for these estimations and calculations are determined by the communities themselves through their "Matrix of Values."

    This addresses the concern raised by contemporary critics that AI systems impose technocratic logic on political decisions. Instead, ISR uses AI to implement community-determined values more effectively and consistently than traditional bureaucratic systems. As Maurizio Lazzarato notes, the key question is not whether to use machines but how to organize human-machine assemblages in ways that serve rather than dominate human agency (2014).

    Part III: ISR and the Restoration of Arendtian Political Action

    Recovering Plurality and Natality

    Arendt's concept of "natality" - the human capacity to begin something new - lies at the heart of her understanding of political action. The ISR framework creates conditions that could restore this capacity by enabling what she called "acting in concert" without the mediation of representatives who inevitably reduce plurality to manageable uniformity.

    The dynamic formation and dissolution of microcommunities mirrors Arendt's understanding of political action as unpredictable and creative. Unlike bureaucratic structures that follow predetermined procedures, microcommunities emerge organically around specific problems and dissolve when those problems are resolved. This preserves space for the unexpected - what Arendt called the "miracle" of new beginnings that characterizes authentic political action.

    Responsibility and the Recovery of Political Agency

    The ISR system directly addresses Arendt's concern that modern society has severed the connection between rights and responsibilities. By making social karma - a measure of past contributions and their outcomes - a central component of political influence, ISR restores what Arendt saw as the essential connection between freedom and responsibility.

    As our framework notes, being useful to someone is the only way to raise ISR. And it is such people who should determine the future of society. This is not a crude utilitarianism but rather an acknowledgment that political freedom emerges through engagement with shared concerns rather than private preferences—a core Arendtian insight.

    The system's requirement for "individual responsibility for both one's actions and inaction" directly implements Arendt's understanding that authentic freedom is not the absence of constraints but the capacity to take responsibility for the consequences of one's actions in a shared world.

    Why ISR Occupies the Center Position:

    Democratic Foundation: Like direct democracy, ISR assumes all members have equal potential to participate and contribute to their communities' futures.

    Meritocratic Elements: Like meritocratic systems, ISR recognizes that expertise, proven track record, and contextual relevance should carry greater weight in specific decisions.

    Addresses Arendt's Concerns: ISR preserves what she valued most—authentic plurality, direct agency, and responsibility—while leveraging expertise through weighted influence rather than technocratic rule.

    Dynamic Weighting: Unlike static meritocracies, ISR weights change per context. Someone expert in labor standards has high influence on workplace issues but normal weight on environmental policy—preserving plurality across domains.

    Community Control: Communities collectively determine how heavily expertise, contribution, and relevance are weighted—preventing expert rule from becoming technocracy.

    Beyond the Social: Restoring the Political

    The ISR framework offers a potential solution to what Arendt identified as the "rise of the social" - the colonization of political space by private, household concerns. By organizing around specific problems rather than identity groups or economic interests, microcommunities create space for what Arendt called "worldly" concerns - issues that transcend private interests and require collective deliberation.

    The AI-mediated formation of microcommunities ensures that participation is based on relevance to specific issues rather than pre-existing social identities or economic positions. This could help create spaces where individuals encounter each other as unique persons rather than representatives of social categories.

    Limitations and Challenges

    The ISR framework, while promising, faces several challenges that must be acknowledged:

    Scale and Complexity: While the system is designed to work well for discrete problems with identifiable stakeholders, it may struggle with large-scale, systemic issues that affect entire societies in diffuse ways.

    Technological Dependence: The system's reliance on AI infrastructure creates new forms of potential dependence and vulnerability that Arendt's framework cannot fully address.

    Psychological Acceptance: Huge social inertia, lack of education, and a habit of irresponsibility can be significant obstacles to mass deployment and adoption.

    Elite Sabotage: Current political elite can perceive the ISR as a threat to their ability to control and rule social processes. It may result in both legal and unlegal resistance to the system implementation.

    Conclusion: Toward Computational Political Action

    The Index of Social Responsibility represents a significant attempt to address the fundamental challenges Arendt identified in modern democratic society. By leveraging AI capabilities to create more responsive, participatory, and accountable forms of political organization, ISR offers a potential pathway beyond both the limitations of representative democracy and the dangers of technocratic governance.

    The framework's emphasis on expertise, responsibility, and contextual relevance aligns with Arendt's core insights about the conditions necessary for authentic political action. Its dynamic, problem-oriented approach to community formation preserves space for the plurality and unpredictability that Arendt saw as essential to human freedom.

    However, realizing this potential will require careful attention to the concerns Arendt raised about the relationship between politics and technology. As she warned, the danger is not technology per se but the tendency to reduce political questions to technical problems that can be solved through expert administration rather than collective deliberation.

    The ISR framework succeeds to the degree that it serves rather than replaces human political capacity. Its value lies not in providing algorithmic solutions to political problems but in creating infrastructure that enables more authentic forms of political participation and personal responsibility.

    As contemporary societies grapple with the limitations of existing democratic institutions, experiments like ISR offer important insights into how technology might serve rather than undermine human political agency. The challenge is to implement such systems in ways that preserve what Arendt called the "space of appearance" - the realm where humans can encounter each other as unique individuals capable of new beginnings.

    The ultimate test of ISR will be whether it enables what Arendt called "power" - the capacity that emerges when people act together for shared concerns - or whether it becomes another form of administration that reduces politics to the management of social and economic processes. Early practical researches suggest promising directions, but much work remains to ensure that computational political action serves rather than supplants the human capacity for freedom that Arendt sought to preserve and restore.
