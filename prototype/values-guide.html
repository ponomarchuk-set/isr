How to Describe Your Community Values for ISR

This guide helps you write a values description that explains the kind of community future you want to create and the kinds of actions that should move that future forward.

What You’re Doing

You are about to describe the kind of community future you want to create and the kinds of actions that should happen in it.

This description becomes a vector, a set of numbers that represents your vision of a good future community. Those vectors are combined with others to form a community values vector.

Core question: “When our community members take some actions, does it move us toward or away from the future we all said we want?”

Single purpose: Aligning community actions with community values.

What This Is Not

  • Not about you: It does not analyze your personality or psychology.
  • Not about judging you: It does not label you as “good” or “bad.”
  • Not about predicting your behavior: It cannot know how you will act.
  • Not about your current life: It is about what you believe should be, not only what exists today.

What You Should Describe

Think about the community you want to see. Describe what should happen in it. The guide is organized around 10 value areas.

1. Freedom and Autonomous Choice

Questions to think about

  • How should people be free to make their own choices?
  • Should people be able to choose their own path, even if others disagree?
  • Who decides what is right for someone — the person themselves, or others?
  • What conditions must exist for people to truly choose freely?

Example: “People should be able to change their mind and choose a different path without punishment.”

2. How We Care for Each Other

Questions to think about

  • How should people relate to each other?
  • Should responsibility extend only to family, or to strangers too?
  • What does caring action look like in community?

Example: “Community should include people unlike us, not only our closest circle.”

3. Safety, Stability, and Basic Order

Questions to think about

  • What makes community life safe and predictable?
  • How should basic needs like food, shelter, and health be provided?
  • What role should clear rules and structures play?

Example: “Everyone should have access to shelter and food, and rules should be clear.”

4. Fair Treatment and Justice

Questions to think about

  • How should resources be distributed?
  • Should equal shares matter more than effort, or vice versa?
  • What should happen when harm is done?

Example: “Basic needs should be met for everyone before anyone gets luxury.”

5. Redemption, Restoration, and Second Chances

Questions to think about

  • When someone makes serious mistakes, what should happen?
  • Can people truly change?
  • What should forgiveness look like in practice?

Example: “The goal should be to restore people to community, not punish forever.”

6. Authentic Expression and Living by Values

Questions to think about

  • Should people be allowed to be fully themselves, even if different?
  • Should people express real beliefs or conform to group norms?
  • How important is it that actions match stated values?

Example: “People should be able to express unpopular views without being silenced.”

7. Protecting the Vulnerable and Stewardship

Questions to think about

  • Who needs special protection?
  • What does community owe to nature, heritage, and future generations?
  • What should be preserved, and what can change?

Example: “We should make decisions while thinking about people not yet born.”

8. Working Together and Sharing

Questions to think about

  • How should important decisions be made?
  • Should resources be shared equally or based on contribution?
  • Should decisions be transparent and explained?

Example: “Important decisions should be made together, not by one person alone.”

9. Spiritual and Religious Practice

Questions to think about

  • What role should spiritual or religious communities play?
  • How should different traditions be respected?
  • What space should community provide for spiritual needs?

Example: “People should be free to practice spiritual or religious traditions.”

10. Digital Rights and Technology

Questions to think about

  • How should personal data and digital privacy be protected?
  • Should technology serve human needs, or should people adapt to it?
  • Who should govern digital systems — companies or communities?

Example: “People should control their own personal data; it should not be extracted from them.”

How to Write Your Description

Length: Aim for 800–1500 words. Less than 400 words is often too thin, and more than 2500 is usually too much.

Style: Write simply and honestly. Phrases like “I think…”, “It seems right that…”, and “In the future community…” work well.

Three key principles

1. Be concrete, not abstract

Weak: “Community should value freedom.”

Strong: “People should be able to choose medical care without others forcing different treatment.”

2. Be honest, not ‘correct’

Describe what you really believe, not what sounds impressive. If you prioritize stability, family, or caution, say so directly.

3. Name real conflicts and choose

When values clash, explain which one matters more to you and why.

Typical conflicts

Freedom vs. safety, truth vs. kindness, individual need vs. group good, justice vs. mercy, tradition vs. change.

Simple Structure You Can Follow

You do not need to use headings, but this structure can make writing easier.

Section 1: Your vision of a good community

Describe what the future community looks like, how people relate, how they decide, and what matters most.

Section 2: Your core beliefs about each domain

Pick the 6–8 domains that matter most to you. For each one, explain what should happen, give an example, and connect it to community action.

Section 3: Where your values conflict

Name 2–4 real tensions, explain which side usually wins for you, and why.

Section 4: Areas you are still thinking about

If you are genuinely uncertain, say so. Uncertainty is still valuable information.

What Happens Next

Vectorization

AI extracts your values and converts them into a numerical representation, described in the guide as a 233-dimensional vector.

Clarification

If parts are unclear, you may receive 1–3 clarifying questions.

Community vector

Your vector is combined with others in your community to create a community-level values vector.

Action assessment

When the community considers an action, the system asks whether it moves the group toward or away from its stated future.

Results

You may see outputs such as alignment, conflict, or tension between values, with community weight explanations.

Important Reminders

  • This describes your vision of the future, not who you are today.
  • Be honest about your actual beliefs. The system is useful only when descriptions are truthful.
  • This is for community alignment, not personal judgment. The purpose is to compare actions to stated values.

Ready to write? Focus on actions and what should happen. Be concrete. Be honest. Name the conflicts.

Questions

What if I’m unsure about something?

Write that you are uncertain and explain why. That is still valuable information.

What if my values contradict each other?

That is normal. Name the contradiction and explain which value usually wins when you must choose.

Should I describe what I do, or what I believe should be?

Describe what you believe should be, not only what you currently do.

Will this be used to judge me?

No. The guide says it is used to assess whether community actions align with community values.

Can I change my description later?

Yes. Values can evolve, and the guide explicitly allows later updates.

End of guide — now describe the community future you want.
